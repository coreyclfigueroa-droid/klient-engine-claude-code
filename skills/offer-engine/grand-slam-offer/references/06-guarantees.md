# 06 — Guarantees

The single biggest objection to any offer is **risk** — risk that it won't work for them. Reversing that risk is one of the most reliable ways to multiply conversions.

Hormozi's claim, drawing on Jason Fladlien: changing the *quality* of a guarantee can move conversions 2–4×. That's larger than almost any copy or pricing change. **Spend a disproportionate amount of time crafting the guarantee.**

You must always hit the guarantee hard, even if it's restrained. Say it boldly. Give the reason why.

---

## The math fear (and why it's misplaced)

The instinct: "If I make a stronger guarantee, won't more people refund and erase my profit?"

Almost never. Run the numbers.

- Without guarantee: 100 sales × 5% refund = 95 net sales
- With strong guarantee: 130 sales × 10% refund = 117 net sales

You netted 23% more revenue *and* served more people.

For a guarantee to actually be net-negative, the increase in refund rate would need to exactly equal the absolute increase in sales (an extra 5% conversion would require an extra 5% refund rate — meaning refunds doubled). That virtually never happens.

**Don't be emotional. Do the math.**

### Caveat — high-cost services

If your cost of fulfillment is high (eats most of your margin), unconditional refund guarantees become risky — you eat the fulfillment cost AND give the money back. Use **conditional guarantees** or **anti-guarantees** (or **implied / performance-based** guarantees) when fulfillment cost is high. Refund-everything guarantees are best for high-margin offers (info products, software, low-cost-to-deliver services).

### Warning — the type of buyer guarantees attract

Strong unconditional guarantees can attract buyers who need a parachute to commit. These can be your worst clients — they don't put in the work and they refund. Mitigate this by tying the guarantee to **action requirements**: "If you do X, Y, Z and don't get the result, I'll refund you / pay you / extend the service." This serves the customer (forces them to do what they need to succeed) AND protects you.

## The four guarantee categories

### 1. UNCONDITIONAL

Pay first, see if you like it, refund if not. Strongest type. Highest conversion lift, highest refund risk.

#### Variations

- Full refund
- 50% refund
- Refund of ad spend / ancillary costs
- "We'll pay for a competitor's program"
- Refund + extra cash on top ("if you're not satisfied, we'll refund $X and add $1,000")

#### Example wording (Jason Fladlien, paraphrased)

"I'm not asking you to decide yes or no today — I'm asking you to make a fully informed decision. The only way to do that is from the inside. Sign up. If at 29 minutes or 29 days from now you're not happy for any reason, write us at [support] and you get your money back. We respond in an average of 61 minutes. I can only make this guarantee because I'm confident in what's inside. When you sign up, you're going to get exactly what you need to [dream outcome]."

This works for low-cost-to-deliver, high-margin offers.

### 2. CONDITIONAL

The guarantee triggers only if specific conditions are met. **Most flexible category. Often the right default.** Lets you protect yourself while still strongly reversing risk.

The rule: conditional guarantees should be *better than money back* — you want the buyer to feel your conviction matches their investment.

#### Conditional outsized refund

"If you do X within Y time and don't see Z result, I'll refund 2× / 3× your money OR pay you $XX,XXX."

Example from book: Fladlien on a $2,997 e-commerce course — "Do this, spend $X on ads, and if you don't make money, I'll buy your store from you for $25,000." Generated $3M in extra sales. Only paid out 10 of the $25K refunds.

Use when margin is high enough to absorb large guaranteed payouts on the few who claim.

#### Conditional service guarantee (Hormozi's favorite)

"You keep [working with us / accessing the program / receiving service] free of charge until you achieve [specific outcome]."

Removes time risk entirely. They can't fail by running out of time — they only fail if they quit. Pair with action requirements (must show up to calls, must complete X, must report data). Real-world results: virtually no one ever claims it. Either they hit the goal, or they're so close that you happily extend, or they quietly drop the request.

#### Conditional modified service guarantee

"If you don't hit X in Y time, you get an additional Y× duration of access free." Similar to above but bounded. Good intermediate step before doing full open-ended service guarantees.

#### Conditional credit-based guarantee

"If you're not satisfied, you get the dollars back as credit toward any other service we offer." Good for upsells where they already like one of your products.

#### Conditional personal-service guarantee

"If you don't hit X, I'll personally work with you 1-on-1 until you do."

Brutally strong. Pair heavily with conditions ("must respond within 24 hours, must use the playbook, must do X, Y, Z") so it only triggers for prospects who actually executed. Becomes more powerful as the operator becomes more edified — Corey at Klient Engine could absolutely deploy this on a high-tier offer.

#### Conditional hotel + airfare reimbursement

"If you attend the workshop and don't find value, we'll reimburse your hotel and airfare on top of your ticket." Great for in-person events. The hotel/airfare line item is a tangible add to the guarantee that feels much bigger than another generic refund.

#### Conditional wage payment

"If you don't find your call with me valuable, I'll pay your hourly rate for the time." Original, memorable, almost never claimed.

#### Conditional release of service

"If you want out, we void any contract / commitment fee." Removes commitment fear. Especially powerful if you don't actually enforce contracts anyway.

#### Conditional delayed second payment

"You won't be billed again until you hit your first outcome." (E.g., first sale, first 5 lbs lost, first listing live.) Forces focus on activation. Makes both you and the client laser-focused on the activating action — strong predictor of long-term retention.

#### Conditional first-outcome guarantee

"If you don't get [first result] in [time], we'll cover [ancillary cost] until you do." E.g., "If you don't make your first sale in 14 days, we cover your ad spend until you do." Aligns incentives, focuses execution.

### 3. ANTI-GUARANTEE

"All sales are final." With a credible *reason why*.

Sounds counterintuitive but works in specific situations:
- The product is highly exclusive / proprietary
- Once seen / used, it cannot be un-seen / un-used (a code, a script, a system)
- Or it's high-touch and requires extensive customization

The trick: lean into it. Make the all-sales-final position itself a credibility move. The implication is that what you have is so valuable and so easy to copy that you have to protect it.

Example wording: "We're going to show you our proprietary process — funnels, ads, exact metrics from our business. Because we're exposing the inner workings, all sales are final. If you're the type of customer who needs a guarantee before taking a jump, you're not the type of customer we want to work with. We want self-starters who follow instructions and aren't looking for an exit before they begin."

This works especially well on high-ticket and B2B. Filters for serious buyers. Removes refund noise from operations.

### 4. IMPLIED (performance-based)

"If we don't perform, we don't get paid." Revenue share, profit share, performance fees, ratchets, bonuses tied to outcomes.

This is technically not a refund-style guarantee — it's a structural alignment of incentives. But it serves the same function: massively reverses buyer risk.

#### Variations

- Pay-per-result ("$X per appointment booked," "$Y per pound lost," "$Z per closed sale")
- Revenue share (% of topline generated by your work)
- Profit share (% of profit)
- Ratchets (10% if over X, 20% if over Y, 30% if over Z)
- Bonuses/triggers ("I get $X when Y happens")

#### When this works best

- You can measure the outcome
- You can trust collection (or build in safeguards)
- The unit economics support it
- The customer values incentive alignment

Often combined with a minimum: "$1,000 per month or 10% of revenue generated, whichever is greater" — covers your fulfillment cost while preserving the upside-aligned story.

This is one of the most underused guarantee structures. Works exceptionally well for agencies, consultants, anyone with quantifiable outcomes. It's the strongest reverse-risk play in the book and produces some of the highest LTV clients (because they only stay if you're delivering — but if you're delivering, they want to keep paying).

## Stacking guarantees

You can layer multiple guarantees. Two common patterns:

### Pattern A — Unconditional + conditional
"Unconditional 30-day no-questions-asked refund. Plus a conditional 90-day triple-money-back guarantee if you do X, Y, Z and don't hit the result."

### Pattern B — Conditional + conditional (sequenced outcomes)
"You'll make $10K by day 60 and $30K by day 90, as long as you do X, Y, Z. If not, [consequence]."

Sequenced conditional guarantees future-pace the buyer into believing the outcome is much more likely — because you're spelling out specific milestones with specific timeframes, with conviction backed by your willingness to put yourself on the hook.

---

## Pro tip — name the guarantee

Don't call it "30-day money-back satisfaction guarantee." That's vanilla and forgettable. Name it.

Generic: "30-day money-back satisfaction guarantee"

Better: "If you don't want to jump into shark-infested waters to get our product back after 30 days, we'll refund every dollar."

Best: "The Club-A-Baby-Seal Guarantee — after 30 days, if you wouldn't club a baby seal to stay on as a customer, you don't pay another penny."

The bigger and more distinctive the name, the more memorable the guarantee, and the more it conveys conviction.

## How to design a guarantee from scratch

1. **Identify the buyer's biggest fear.** What do they *not* want to happen if they pay you? Time wasted? Money wasted? Looking foolish? Effort with no result?
2. **Reverse that fear into the consequence clause.** "If you do X and don't get Y, then [the thing that addresses their specific fear]."
3. **Pick the type** based on your margin profile:
   - High margin (info, software, low-touch service) → can do unconditional or strong conditional
   - Tight margin (high-cost fulfillment) → conditional with action requirements, or anti-guarantee, or performance-based
4. **Add specific conditions** if needed to filter buyers and protect yourself
5. **Add teeth** — "we'll refund / pay / continue working / give you $X / etc."
6. **Name it** memorably
7. **State it boldly** in the offer

---

## Hormozi's recommendation for getting started

Start with a **conditional service guarantee** ("we keep working with you until X") or a **performance-based partnership** (rev share / pay-per-result). These commit you to results, eliminate refund risk to you, and force you to actually deliver. Once you're confident in your delivery, you can experiment with less restrictive guarantees if you want to scale conversion volume further.

For Corey: high-ticket DFY engagements scream for performance-based / equity guarantees. Skill packs and community tiers can use conditional service guarantees ("ship your first piece in 7 days or your money back" / "first month free if you don't hit X"). Each tier of his stack should have a guarantee designed for that tier's economics.
