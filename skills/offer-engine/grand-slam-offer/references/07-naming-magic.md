# 07 — Naming (the MAGIC Formula)

The name is the wrapper. The wrapper determines whether anyone unwraps the gift.

A great offer with a flat name fatigues fast and converts low. The same offer with a sharp name converts at multiples and runs longer in market. Same fulfillment, same delivery, same price — different name, different business.

---

## The MAGIC formula

```
M — Magnet — the reason why (promotion, discount, season, occasion)
A — Avatar — who it's for (specific person/role/situation)
G — Goal — the dream outcome (vivid, specific, tangible)
I — Interval — the time window (duration to result, or duration of program)
C — Container — the format word (system, blueprint, sprint, intensive…)
```

Not all five every time. **Three to five is the sweet spot.** All five usually makes the name too long. Brevity > completeness — the test is what reads punchy out loud.

You don't have to use them in MAGIC order. Reorder for sound.

---

## The five components, expanded

### M — Magnet (the reason why)

Tells the prospect why this offer exists right now / why they should respond. Even a thin reason works (think: fraternity-party-planner energy — "we're throwing this party because Jeff got his wisdom teeth out"). The brain just needs a reason.

Examples:
- Free
- 88% off
- Giveaway
- Spring / Summer / Back-to-School
- Grand Opening / New Management / Anniversary
- Halloween / New Year / Valentine's
- Launch / Beta

This is also the urgency hook — the magnet often implies a time window.

### A — Avatar (who)

Calls out who it's for and (by exclusion) who it's not for. **Specificity is the lever.** The more granular the avatar, the higher the price you can charge AND the higher the response rate.

Examples:
- Busy moms
- Brick-and-mortar businesses
- Salon owners
- Retired athletes
- Brooklyn busy executives
- Behave dentists
- Rolling Hills moms
- AI agency operators
- Gym owners with $30K+ MRR

Local-business note: get hyper-local. Not "Chicago" — "Hinsdale." Not "Baltimore" — "Towson." Submarket targeting outconverts metro-level targeting consistently.

### G — Goal (the dream outcome)

The vivid result. Single word or short phrase. Event, feeling, experience, or outcome — anything the avatar wants.

Examples:
- Pain-free
- Celebrity smile
- First place
- Never out of breath
- Perfect product
- Grand slam offer
- Little black dress
- Double your profit
- First client
- High ticket
- 7-figure
- $100K
- First $10K month

Specificity wins here too. "Lose weight" < "Lose 20 lbs" < "Fit into your wedding dress."

### I — Interval (the time)

Duration. Either how long the program runs, or how long until the result.

Examples:
- 60 minutes
- 4 hours
- 21 days
- 30 days
- 6 weeks
- 8 weeks
- 90 days
- 12 weeks
- 6 months

**Compliance note:** if your goal includes a *quantifiable claim* (income, weight loss, etc.), most ad platforms (Meta especially) won't approve a name that pairs the claim with a duration — it reads as a guarantee. Workaround: use the interval with non-quantifiable goals ("First Sale in 10 Days" is fine; "$10,000 in 10 Days" gets flagged). Use the bigger paired version on owned channels (sales pages, emails, organic).

### C — Container (the format)

The wrapper word that signals "this is a system / bundle / program — not a commoditized service." The container is what lets the offer escape comparison.

Categories:
- **Speed-implying**: Sprint, Fast-Track, Shortcut, Quickstart
- **Depth-implying**: Intensive, Deep Dive, Mastermind, Bootcamp, Incubator
- **Transformation-implying**: Transformation, Comeback, Reset, Rebirth
- **Event-implying**: Summit, Workshop, Masterclass, Experience, Launch, Liftoff
- **Mechanical-implying**: Blueprint, Playbook, System, Method, Framework, Engine, Machine
- **Energy-implying**: Catapult, Accelerator, Slingshot, Explosion, Attack, Assault
- **Solution-implying**: Solution, Hack, Cheat Code, Detox

Container choice signals tone. "Detox" feels different from "Mastermind" feels different from "Sprint" — pick one that matches the avatar's vocabulary and the offer's intensity.

## Pro tip — rhyme

Rhyming names stick. Not required — don't force it — but when it works, it triples memorability.

- Six-Pack Fast Track
- Five-Day Bookprint Sprint
- Marriage Thrive Deep Dive
- 12-Week Twerk Shortcut
- 12-Month No-Debt Reset

Tool: rhyming dictionary online. Plug in the goal/container word and scan for adjacent words that fit.

## Pro tip — alliteration

Easier than rhyming. Repeat starting sounds across two or more words.

- Make Money Masterclass
- Change-Your-Life Challenge
- Big Booty Bootcamp
- Debt Detox
- Real Estate Reset
- Life Coach Liftoff

Same rule: don't force it. If it lands, it lands.

## Apply MAGIC to bonuses too

Don't stop at the headline. Run every sub-offer and every bonus through MAGIC. A bonus called "Grocery Calculator" feels like an afterthought; "Bargain-Mom 5-Minute Grocery Genius Calculator" feels like a $497 standalone. Same asset, different perceived value.

## Variation — managing offer fatigue

Offers fatigue, especially in local markets where you can saturate the entire addressable population for ~$10K of ad spend. Variation is how you keep an offer running for years.

**The variation order — always go in this sequence, top to bottom, lightest to heaviest. Don't change the deeper layer until you've exhausted the shallower one.**

| Order | What you change | Operational cost |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | The creative (images, video) | Trivial |
| 2 | The body copy of the ad | Trivial |
| 3 | The headline / wrapper of the offer | Low |
| 4 | The duration of the offer | Low-moderate |
| 5 | The enhancer (free / discount component) | Moderate |
| 6 | The entire monetization model | High — last resort |

The pattern in practice: you'll spend most of your time iterating layers 1 and 2. When those stop refreshing the offer, change the wrapper (the name). "6-Week Stress Release Challenge" becomes "42-Day Relaxing Holidays Reset." Same fulfillment. New name. Fresh response.

Once an offer is monetizing well, **rarely change the underlying mechanism.** Use your entrepreneurial appetite for novelty on the wrapper, not the engine. Changing the engine costs operations and usually loses traction.

### Variation in practice — same offer, four wrappers across a year

- January: "Free 6-Week Lean-by-New-Year Challenge"
- February: "Valentine's Lover Body 42-Day Transformation"
- March: "Sexy by Spring 6-Week Reset"
- April: "Fools-in-Love April 21-Day Tone-Up"

Same fulfillment. Same price. Four fresh wrappers over four months. Each one converts because it's new, even though the engine underneath is identical.

---

## Local business note

Local businesses have a smaller TAM, so offers fatigue faster. The MAGIC variation framework is doubly important here. If you're in a Klient Engine context working with local clients (gyms, dental, salon, etc.), expect to rotate the wrapper monthly or bi-monthly. National/online offers can run the same wrapper for a year or more before fatigue.

---

## Diagnostic — when a name isn't working

Run it through these checks:

- **Does it contain at least 3 of MAGIC?** If not, it's probably too thin.
- **Could a stranger guess what it does and who it's for in <2 seconds?** If not, it's too clever or too vague.
- **Does it sound like the avatar?** Names written in operator-language convert worse than names in customer-language.
- **Does it have a distinctive container word?** Generic words like "Course" or "Program" usually underperform punchier containers.
- **Out loud — does it have rhythm?** If it stumbles to say, it stumbles to remember.

---

## Final note — how to test names

Generate 3–5 candidate names per offer. Run two or three of the best in your initial campaign. Track conversions. Note the winner — that becomes your control. Then test new names against it over time.

Don't get emotional about which one wins. Some names take off for reasons that aren't intuitive. Stop trying to predict it; just test, measure, and ride the winner.
