# 04 — Trim & Stack

You now have a flood of solutions and delivery vehicles from `03-problems-solutions-delivery.md`. This step turns that raw material into an actual deliverable offer that's profitable to fulfill and irresistible to buy.

## The fundamental tension — sales-to-fulfillment continuum

Easy to sell ↔ Easy to fulfill is a continuum. They pull against each other:

- **Maximum done-for-you, every problem solved personally** = trivially easy to sell, brutal to fulfill at scale
- **Pure DIY product** = hard to sell, trivial to fulfill

Your job is to find the sweet spot. Two practical principles:

1. **Create flow, monetize flow, then add friction.** Generate demand first by overdelivering. Validate that what you have actually sells. Once cash is flowing in, optimize fulfillment by replacing high-cost components with lower-cost equivalents that deliver the same perceived value.

2. **For your first Grand Slam Offer, err toward overdelivery.** Make some sales. Get cash. Then systematize. Trying to optimize fulfillment before you have proof of demand is premature optimization.

### The Hormozi gym example

Started by flying to gyms personally for 21-day in-person turnarounds. Took on all the risk, did all the work, paid his own travel, kept the upfront cash from the launch. Selling $100K/mo, but it didn't scale and was killing him.

Then a gym owner forced him to remote-launch instead. Same dream outcome, dramatically different delivery. Worked even better. Suddenly it scaled — went from 8 launches/mo to thousands of gyms enrolled.

**The promise stayed the same. The how changed.** That's the trim/stack move at scale.

## Step 5A — Trim

Take the full list of solutions × delivery vehicles. Score each on two axes:

- **Cost to deliver** (your time, money, complexity to fulfill)
- **Value to the prospect** (run through the value equation — does it move dream outcome, likelihood, time, or effort/sacrifice?)

Then cut in this order:

1. **Cut first:** high cost to deliver + low perceived value. Easy decision, no debate.
2. **Cut next:** low cost + low value. Clutter — they don't help and they dilute the stack.
3. **Keep:** low cost + high value. Always. These are the bonuses and core stack items that make the offer feel insane without breaking your fulfillment.
4. **Keep selectively:** high cost + high value. These are your differentiators and signature components, but use sparingly. Reserve high-touch / 1-on-1 / done-for-you for the pieces that genuinely require it. Replace anywhere you can with a one-to-many version that delivers similar perceived value.

### The "is there a lesser version?" question

For any high-cost component, ask: "Is there a smaller-scale version of this that delivers most of the perceived value?" Step backward one level at a time:

- Live in their house with them ($∞) → 1-on-1 in-person sessions ($$$$)
- 1-on-1 in-person sessions → 1-on-1 Zoom ($$$)
- 1-on-1 Zoom → Small-group Zoom ($$)
- Small-group Zoom → Recorded video + Q&A async ($)
- Recorded video → Self-serve playbook ($¢)

You don't have to go all the way down. Stop at the level where cost is manageable AND perceived value is still high enough to keep the offer compelling. Or — if the high-cost version is genuinely uniquely valuable — keep it and price the whole offer accordingly.

## Step 5B — Stack

Bundle the kept items into a presentable offer with named sub-offers and dollar values.

### Bundle structure

For each cluster of related solutions, build a **named sub-offer** with:

- A **name** (use the MAGIC formula from `07-naming-magic.md` — outcome-driven, with rhyme/alliteration if it lands)
- A **promise** (the specific outcome the sub-offer delivers)
- A **dollar value** (justified by what it would cost the buyer in time/money to get this elsewhere or build themselves)
- A **bullet list** of the actual components inside

### The Hormozi 6-week fitness offer (reference example)

After the full stack/trim process, his $599 boot camp offer looked like this:

| Sub-offer | Components | Value |
|---|---|---|
| **Foolproof Bargain Grocery System** — save hundreds/mo on food, less time than current shopping | 1-on-1 nutrition orientation, recorded grocery tour, DIY calculator, weekly list, bargain shopping training, buddy system, pre-made Instacart carts, weekly text check-in | $1,000 |
| **Ready-in-5-Minute Busy Parent Cooking Guide** — eat healthy with no time | 1-on-1 orientation, meal prep instructions, DIY calculator, weekly meal prep, buddy system, snacks-in-5 guide, weekly tag-for-feedback post | $600 |
| **Personalized Lick-Your-Fingers Meal Plan** — easier and cheaper than what you cheat with | 1-on-1 orientation, personalized plan, 5-min shake guide, 5-min budget lunches, 5-min budget dinners, family meals, daily meal pic check-in, 1-on-1 feedback (upsell) | $500 |
| **Fat-Burning Workouts** — proven to burn more fat than going alone, adjusted for any level | (workout system) | $699 |
| **Ultimate Tone-Up While You Travel** — eating + workout blueprint, no equipment, no guilt | (travel system) | $199 |
| **Never-Fall-Off Accountability System** — works without your permission | (accountability system) | $1,000 |
| **Live-It-Up While Slimming Down Eating Out System** — freedom to eat out and not be the odd man out | (eating out system) | $349 |

**Total stated value: $4,351. Price: $599.**

Three things to notice:
1. **Every sub-offer is named with the outcome baked in**, not the mechanism. ("Foolproof Bargain Grocery System" not "Grocery shopping module.")
2. **The value totals dwarf the price.** This is the price-to-value gap that makes the offer irresistible.
3. **Each sub-offer maps to specific problems** from the problem list, often crushing 4–6 problems at once.

The actual *fulfillment* of this offer is the same gym membership it always would have been — but bundled and presented this way, it sells at 6× the typical $99/mo gym rate, with prospects feeling they're getting a steal.

### Dollar-value attribution rules

Don't make up numbers — but also don't leave value implicit. For each sub-offer / bonus, ask:

- What would this cost the buyer to get from a competitor or specialist?
- What would it cost them in time × hourly value to build themselves?
- What's the financial outcome it produces for them (in dollars saved, earned, or risk avoided)?

Pick the most defensible of those numbers. Round up slightly but stay credible. Numbers like $1,997 or $497 read more credibly than $2,000 or $500 — they imply pricing precision rather than round invention.

When in doubt, undervalue rather than overvalue. The total value should still be at least 5–10× the price. If it can be 20–50×, that's better. The Grand Slam Offer in the book's case study had a stated value 7.3× the price.

## What does the stacked offer accomplish?

When you've trimmed and stacked correctly, the offer does three things at once:

1. **Solves every perceived problem** — not most, every one. The bundle's components map directly back to the problem list.
2. **Gives YOU conviction the offer is one-of-a-kind** — because it literally is. Nobody else in the market is bundling these specific components in this specific way at this price.
3. **Makes comparison impossible** — a prospect can no longer hold your offer next to a generic competitor and price-shop. There's no apples-to-apples.

That's the entire point of the Grand Slam construction: get out of the price comparison game by becoming a category of one.

## The trim/stack output

By the end of this stage you have:

- 5–8 named sub-offers, each with a one-line outcome promise, a dollar value, and a bullet list of contents
- A total stated value (sum of sub-offer values)
- The price you're going to charge (premium, anchored against value)
- A clear price-to-value gap (ideally 5–20×+)

This is the **core offer**. From here, the next layers are enhancement: scarcity, urgency, bonuses (which add even more value on top), guarantees, and the name. Each is a multiplier on this foundation. None of them will save a weak core.

If the core stack is weak after trim/stack, **don't move forward to enhancements.** Go back to step 2 and list more problems, or back to step 4 and brainstorm more delivery vehicles. Enhancements amplify what's already there — they don't create what isn't.
