# 03 — Problems, Solutions & Delivery Vehicles

The five-step process for building the *core* of the Grand Slam Offer. This is the meat of the book — what the offer actually IS before you start enhancing it.

## The five steps

1. Identify the dream outcome
2. List every problem standing between the prospect and that outcome
3. Flip every problem into a solution
4. Brainstorm delivery vehicles for each solution (using the Delivery Cube)
5. Trim and stack (covered in `04-trim-and-stack.md`)

Don't shortcut. Each step feeds the next. Skipping problem listing makes the solutions list shallow. Skipping the delivery brainstorm collapses the offer back to whatever you'd default to.

---

## Step 1 — Identify the dream outcome

The vivid, specific end state. **Not the membership. Not the platform. The outcome.**

Examples:
- ❌ "$99/mo gym membership"
- ✅ "Lose 20 lbs in 6 weeks"
- ❌ "Marketing agency services"
- ✅ "20 booked appointments in 30 days, or you don't pay"
- ❌ "Relationship coaching subscription"
- ✅ "An amazing, loving relationship within 90 days"

The dream outcome must include:
- A **measurable result** (or specific qualitative state)
- A **timeframe** (when applicable)
- The **buyer's actual desire**, not the operator's preferred metric

If the dream outcome is fuzzy, every downstream step becomes fuzzy. Force it into one sentence the prospect would actually say.

---

## Step 2 — List every problem

Walk through what the prospect has to do, in sequence, to reach the dream outcome. For each step, list every problem that could derail them.

**Don't be efficient — be exhaustive.** More problems = more solutions = more bonus surface area = bigger value stack.

### The four-bucket framework

For every step in the journey, problems usually cluster into four buckets that map to the value equation:

1. **Dream outcome** — "this won't be financially worth it"
2. **Likelihood of achievement** — "this won't work for me specifically; I won't stick with it; external factors will get in the way"
3. **Effort & sacrifice** — "this will be hard / confusing / I won't like it / I'll suck at it"
4. **Time** — "this will take too long / I'm too busy / it won't be convenient"

### Worked example — fitness/weight loss offer

Sequence: buy healthy food → cook healthy food → eat healthy food → exercise regularly → travel without falling off → maintain socially → stick with it long-term

For just the *first* step (buying healthy food), the four buckets produce:

| Bucket | Problem |
|---|---|
| Effort | Buying healthy food is hard, confusing, I won't like it, I'll suck at it |
| Time | Buying healthy food takes too much time |
| Dream outcome | Buying healthy food is expensive |
| Likelihood | I won't be able to keep this up; family needs will derail me; if I travel, I won't know what to buy |

Now do that for *every* sequential step. A single offer can easily generate 30–60 distinct problems. That's the right order of magnitude. **No wonder most people fail at their goals — and that's the good news for you, the offer-builder.** Every problem is a place to add value.

### When you can't think of more problems

Add specificity. Walk through the journey *literally* — open the cabinet, drive to the store, stand in the aisle. The more concrete the visualization, the more problems surface. If the offer is for a specific avatar, walk through *their* day, not a generic one.

---

## Step 3 — Flip every problem into a solution

Mechanical step. For each problem, write the inverse as a "How to..." statement.

| Problem | Solution |
|---|---|
| Buying healthy food is hard and confusing | How to make buying healthy food easy and enjoyable, even if you're a busy parent |
| Cooking healthy food takes too much time | How to cook healthy meals in under 5 minutes |
| Healthy food is expensive | How to buy healthy food for less than your current grocery bill |
| Family will derail my eating | How to feed yourself and your family from one shopping trip |
| Travel kills the diet | How to eat clean while traveling |
| Cooking is complicated | How anyone can enjoy cooking healthy meals easily |

The solution list is, in essence, **a checklist of what your offer is going to do.** You're not yet committing to *how* — that's the next step. You're committing to the *what*.

### Why every problem matters

You will solve **every** problem. Not most. Every one.

The story from the book: Hormozi initially refused to take weight-loss clients who ate out. One sale needed money badly enough to compromise — built a one-time "eating out guide" for that client to close the sale. From that point on, every prospect who said "but what about eating out?" got an instant answer. Over time, every one-thing-objection got its own solution document. Sales conversions climbed massively.

**The single missing problem is often the single reason a prospect doesn't buy.** Solve all of them.

## Step 4 — Brainstorm delivery vehicles (the *how*)

Now decide *how* you're going to solve each problem. This is where divergent thinking matters most.

For every solution, brainstorm multiple ways you *could* deliver it — not what you'd default to, but the full range. You're going to trim later, but the trimming step needs raw material.

### The Delivery Cube — six dimensions to vary on

For any solution, vary it across:

#### A. Level of personal attention
- 1-on-1
- Small group
- One-to-many

#### B. Level of effort expected from the buyer
- DIY (do it themselves)
- Done-with-you (you teach them how)
- Done-for-you (you do it, period)

#### C. If live, what medium
- In person
- Phone
- Email
- Text / chat
- Zoom
- Livestream

#### D. If recorded, how to consume
- Audio
- Video
- Written
- Interactive

#### E. Speed of response / availability
- 24/7
- Business hours
- Within 5 min
- Within 1 hour
- Within 24 hours
- Async only

#### F. The 10× / ⅒ thought experiment
- If they paid 10× the price, what would you provide?
- If they paid ⅒ the price, how would you still make them succeed?

Stretching mind in either direction surfaces options you'd never default to.

### Worked example — buying healthy food problem

Solving "buying healthy food is hard, confusing":

**1-on-1 options:**
- a. In-person grocery shopping where I take them and teach
- b. Personalized grocery list built with them
- c. Done-for-you shopping (I buy and deliver)
- d. In-person off-site nutrition orientation
- e. Tech support text line during shopping
- f. Phone call during their first shopping trip

**Small-group options:**
- a. Group grocery store tour
- b. Group personalized list workshop
- c. Group buying coordinated delivery
- d. Off-site small group orientation

**One-to-many options:**
- a. Live livestream of me shopping with Q&A
- b. Recorded grocery store tour as a reference asset
- c. DIY grocery calculator tool
- d. Pre-built weekly grocery lists per plan
- e. Buddy system — pair clients to shop together
- f. Pre-made Instacart carts ready to one-click

This is **17 ways** to solve a single problem. Now do this for every problem on the list.

The point isn't to do all of them. It's to see the full menu so the trim/stack step (`04-trim-and-stack.md`) has real choices.

### Why one-to-many is gold

The delivery vehicles that consistently produce the highest value at lowest cost are **high-cost-to-create / zero-cost-to-deliver one-to-many assets.** Calculators, templates, swipe files, recorded trainings, scripts, frameworks. They take effort to build once, then deliver infinite value at zero marginal cost. Software is the extreme case — pure margin once built.

When designing the offer, look hardest for opportunities to convert a 1-on-1 service into a one-to-many asset. The offer doesn't have to be all one-to-many — but every component that *can* become one-to-many should.

---

## The "solve every problem" principle (cannot be overstated)

The book's punchline on this: **you must resolve every obstacle a buyer believes they will have to convert the highest amount of people.** Not solving every problem doesn't mean you'll get zero sales — it means you'll leave money on the table from the people who were one objection away.

The four-bucket exercise (effort, time, dream outcome, likelihood) ensures you don't miss an obvious category. The "every step in the journey" walk-through ensures you don't miss a stage. The delivery brainstorm ensures you have multiple solution options to pick the cheapest, highest-value way to crush each one.

If during sales conversations you keep hearing the same objection, that's a missing solution in the offer. Add it as a bonus or as part of the core stack. Now nobody asks again.

---

## Output of this stage

By the end of step 4, you should have:

- A one-sentence dream outcome
- 30–60 problems listed in sequence across the buyer's journey
- 30–60 corresponding solutions (each as a "How to..." statement)
- Multiple delivery vehicle options for each solution

This is raw material — not the offer yet. The next step is to trim and stack it into something deliverable. See `04-trim-and-stack.md`.
