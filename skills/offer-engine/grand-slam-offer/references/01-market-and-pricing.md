# 01 — Market & Pricing

The first two layers under any Grand Slam Offer. If the market is wrong or the pricing posture is wrong, nothing downstream saves the offer.

## Part A — Picking / validating the market

### The fundamental rule

A Grand Slam Offer to the wrong audience falls on deaf ears. **You're not creating demand — you're channeling existing demand.** The offer is a vehicle for a desire that already exists in the market.

### The hot dog / starving crowd parable

Marketing prof asks: if you could only have one advantage running a hot dog stand — location, quality, low prices, best taste — which would you pick? Answer: **a starving crowd.** Bad hot dogs at bad prices in a bad location still sell out if it's 2 a.m. outside a bar. Demand beats everything else.

### The four market criteria (run any niche through this)

A workable market needs all four:

1. **Massive pain.** They don't want, they need. The pain is the pitch — if you can articulate the pain accurately, they almost always buy. Pain proportional to price you can charge.
2. **Purchasing power.** They have or can access the money. (The cautionary tale: helping unemployed people fix resumes — perfect pain, zero ability to pay.)
3. **Easy to target.** They cluster somewhere — associations, lists, social groups, channels, search behavior. If finding them is needles-in-haystacks, the offer dies in distribution.
4. **Growing.** Tailwind, not headwind. (Cautionary tale: the friend selling software to newspapers — great product, great offer, great sales skill, dying market. He couldn't win until he switched markets entirely.)

### The three eternal markets

Health, wealth, relationships. Pain in these is permanent. Pick a sub-niche inside one. Prefer sub-niches with more pain, more money, easier targeting, and growth.

Example: relationship coaching — second-half-of-life coaching beats college-student coaching on all four. More pain (loneliness near end of life), more money (accumulated wealth), easier to target (senior groups, AARP-adjacent), growing (more people turning 65 each year than 20).

### Order of importance: market > offer > persuasion

A great market with a mediocre offer and bad persuasion still makes money. A normal market needs a Grand Slam Offer to win. A normal market with a normal offer requires exceptional persuasion to win — the hardest path.

You can't be in a *bad* market — that's the only hard rule. Normal is fine. Most successful businesses are in normal markets.

### Commit to the niche

Don't niche-hop. The grass isn't greener. All markets have unpleasant characteristics. Switching every time you fail resets the learning curve. Pick one, commit until you have a Grand Slam Offer working in it.

### Riches in the niches — pricing power of specificity

Same product, different niching, dramatically different price:

- "Time management" — $19
- "Time management for sales professionals" — $99
- "Time management for outbound B2B sales reps" — $499
- "Time management for outbound B2B power tools and gardening sales reps" — $1,997

The product can be 90% identical. The price difference comes from how specifically the buyer feels seen. The narrowest version makes the buyer think "this is exactly for me" and pay accordingly.

**Rule for businesses under $10M/yr:** niche down. Almost always more profit, easier messaging, easier sales. Only broaden when you've capped out the TAM (most never do — 99.6% of businesses don't even hit $10M).

## Part B — Pricing posture

### The thesis

**Charge a premium. Always.** There's no strategic benefit to being the second-cheapest in a market — only to being the most expensive. Premium pricing isn't greedy; it's the only way to build a business that can deliver real value over time.

### Why most businesses underprice

The default pricing process: look at competitors, take the average, go slightly below to "stay competitive," provide what they provide plus a little more. The big secret — those competitors are dead broke. Copying them is copying failure. This pricing process is "more for less" and ends in market efficiency where margins evaporate and everyone barely survives.

### The virtuous cycle of price

When you **lower** prices:
- Decreases client emotional investment (it didn't cost much, so they don't show up)
- Decreases perceived value (cheap = not good)
- Decreases client results (they don't follow through because they don't value it)
- Attracts the worst clients (price-shoppers, never satisfied)
- Destroys margin → no money to invest in service, people, growth, infrastructure
- Your service degrades because there's nothing left to invest with
- You join the race to the bottom

When you **raise** prices:
- Increases emotional investment ("I paid real money for this; I'm doing the work")
- Increases perceived value (expensive = must be good)
- Increases client results (because they're invested)
- Attracts the best clients (easiest to satisfy, most likely to perceive value, often cheapest to fulfill)
- Multiplies margin → invest in systems, hire well, improve experience, scale
- You can sustain and improve over the long haul

### The wine study (price IS value)

Blind taste test, three wines at three price points — researchers gave participants the *same wine* all three times. Participants consistently rated the "expensive" one as best. **Price itself increases perceived value.** This is not a metaphor — it's a measurable cognitive effect.

Implication: raising your price doesn't just capture more revenue, it directly increases what your customer experiences.

### "It must hurt a little"

Those who pay the most pay the most attention. A price that stings forces focus. If you actually care about your customers' results, you should price high enough to make them invest. Cheap pricing isn't kindness — it's a form of sabotage.

### The conviction requirement

Premium pricing requires conviction. You can't ask for someone's annual salary if you don't deeply believe — based on real reps — that they'll get it back many times over. Conviction comes from delivery experience: doing the thing enough times that you *know* it works. Without that, premium pricing collapses into hype.

### Goal of pricing

**Not** to get the most customers. **To make the most money** with the right customers, with the margin to deliver real value and the room to grow. A grand slam offer at premium price beats a commoditized offer at low price on every dimension — revenue, profit, fulfillment quality, customer outcomes, business sustainability.

### The Hormozi gym example (pricing reference)

When he entered the gym-owner consulting space:
- Low-priced competitors: $500/mo full marketing service
- Single high-priced competitor: $5,000
- Hormozi's price: $16,000 for a 16-week intensive (3× the highest-priced player, 32× the lowest), with 35% upsold to $42,000/yr for three years
- Average gym owner take-home: $35,280/yr
- He was selling *more than half their annual salary* to a single program
- It worked because his conviction was stronger than their skepticism — proven by results: 11-month average outcomes of +$239K topline, +$160K recurring, profit growing 3.1×

### Audit checklist for any existing price

When auditing whether a price is right, ask:
- Is the value-to-price gap visible? (If price < value × 10, you can probably raise.)
- Is the price competitive with the market or above it? (Above is correct.)
- Does the pricing match the conviction? (If the price feels high to *you*, you need either more reps or more value-stacking — not a lower price.)
- Does the operator have margin to invest in delivery? (If not, raise the price.)
- Are clients getting the result and showing up invested? (If under-investing, the price is too low.)
