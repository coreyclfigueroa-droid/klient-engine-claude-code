# 05 — Scarcity, Urgency & Bonuses

The first three enhancement layers on top of the core offer. These shift the supply/demand curve in your favor — same offer, more conversions, often at higher prices.

The frame: **all marketing exists to influence supply and demand.** Marketing artificially increases demand. Scarcity decreases (perceived) supply. Urgency forces decision timing. Bonuses inflate the price-to-value gap. Used together, they turn a good offer into a frenzied one.

The key principle behind all three: **desire comes from not getting what you want.** Naval Ravikant: "Desire is a contract you make with yourself to be unhappy until you get what you want." If you fully satisfy demand, desire dies. If you keep desire slightly under-satisfied, it intensifies.

This means: **sell fewer units than you can.** Skim the top of the demand pyramid at premium prices. Leave demand on the table. The unmet demand becomes pressure on the next launch.

---

## SCARCITY — limit supply

Scarcity = limited *quantity*. Decreases perceived availability, which increases perceived value AND triggers loss-aversion (fear of missing out is stronger than desire for gain).

### The three types

#### 1. Limited supply of seats / slots
Cap how many you'll take, total or per period.

#### 2. Limited supply of bonuses
The core offer is unlimited but the bonuses attached are capped. (Useful when you can't credibly cap the core.)

#### 3. Never available again
One-time release. Strongest but only usable once per launch.

### Three honest scarcity tactics for ongoing use

#### a. Total business cap
"I only take X clients total." When at capacity, run a waitlist. Periodically open 10-20% new slots; close again. Works best for high-tier services.

#### b. Growth rate cap
"I only take X new clients per week." Concrete, easy to use in sales conversations: "We've already booked 3 of this week's 5 spots. You can take one of the remaining two or wait until next week."

#### c. Cohort cap
"We start X clients per cohort, four times a year." Operationally clean — creates onboarding cadence — and gives sales legitimate scarcity. The longer between cohorts, the stronger the urgency.

### The honest scarcity move (always available)

You probably can't actually serve unlimited clients next week. Whatever your real cap is — say it. "We're 81% to capacity this month." That single line moves people off the fence. The closer to "full," the faster the remaining slots disappear. **You define where "full" is.** It's an honest number based on what you can actually deliver well.

### Critical rules for scarcity

- **Sell out.** Better to consistently sell out at a smaller cap than to overshoot and fail to create scarcity. Compounds in effectiveness over time as the market sees you sell out repeatedly.
- **Announce the sellout.** Part of what makes scarcity work is the social proof of others having bought. Tell the people who *didn't* get in. Next launch they'll move faster.
- **Don't fake it.** Once you're caught, the strategy is dead. Honest scarcity scales; fake scarcity destroys trust.

### Pro tip — extreme scarcity for maximum profit

If you don't hate money: create a tiny, very limited 1-on-1 access tier at a very high price. Cap it brutally low (3–10 slots). Tell people. The 1% of the 1% will adjust their lives to take the slot. Pick a delivery medium you don't hate (Zoom calls, voice memos, async DMs — whatever doesn't drain you). This is one of the highest-leverage moves in the book.

### Pro tip — once you're out, you can't come back

Add this clause to high-tier services: if a client leaves, they can't return. Makes people think twice about leaving. Works best on small/exclusive tiers. Loses teeth when groups get too big.

## URGENCY — limit time

Urgency = limited *time window*. Forces decision timing without changing what's offered.

### The four types

#### 1. Cohort-based rolling urgency
"We start a new group every Monday. Sign up today and you're in this week's group, otherwise next week." Operationally clean. Even unlimited-capacity businesses can use this.

Variation: "I had someone drop out — there's an opening for this week if you want it."

#### 2. Rolling seasonal urgency
"Our New Year promo ends January 30. February: Valentine's promo. March: Sexy by Spring." Same underlying offer rewrapped seasonally. Real dates, real cutoffs. Powerful for local businesses where offer fatigue happens fast.

#### 3. Pricing/bonus-based urgency
"The discount runs until [date], we change them every 4 weeks." Or "If you sign up today, I'll add [bonus] worth $X — that bonus comes off after this week." Lets you maintain integrity ("we don't refuse customers after the date") while still creating urgency around price/bonus terms.

#### 4. Exploding opportunity
For arbitrage/timing-sensitive plays — "every day you wait, the opportunity decays." Real estate flips, crypto launches, new ad platforms before they're saturated. Only works when the urgency is real.

### Critical insight on urgency: cleaning your pipeline

When you raise prices, **always announce it.** "Prices going up on [date]." The people on the fence finally jump. You get a pipeline-cleaning revenue spike from prospects who were going to buy eventually. This is one of the most reliable money-printing moves available — costs nothing, requires no new offer, just an announced price change.

### What if you lose sales by turning business away?

You won't. The math: in launch-style campaigns, **50–60% of total sales happen in the last 4 hours of the last day.** That's the urgency cliff doing the work. People who didn't buy in the urgency window weren't going to buy without it either.

## BONUSES — inflate the price-to-value gap

Bonuses don't change the core offer. They inflate the *stated value* of what the buyer receives without dropping the price. Because the price stays anchored, every additional bonus widens the price-to-value gap until it snaps the buyer's resistance.

This is why every infomercial does "But wait, there's more!" forever. They're not being cheesy — they're using the most reliable persuasion mechanism in direct response.

### Bonus presentation rules — 11 essentials

1. **Always offer them.** No exceptions on a Grand Slam Offer.
2. **Give each a specific name with a benefit baked in.** ("Bargain Grocery Calculator," not "Spreadsheet.")
3. **For each bonus, walk through:**
   - a. How it relates to a specific obstacle
   - b. What it is
   - c. How you discovered it / what it took to create it (creates value perception)
   - d. How it specifically improves their life (faster, easier, less effort — value equation)
4. **Provide proof.** A stat, a past client, a personal story.
5. **Paint the picture.** Vivid mental image of their life *after* using this bonus.
6. **Always assign a dollar value.** Justify it.
7. **Tools and checklists > more trainings.** Lower effort to consume = higher perceived value.
8. **Each bonus crushes a specific objection.** Match bonuses to the obstacles surfaced in the problems list.
9. **Address the next problem before they encounter it.** What's the question they'll have *after* they get the core thing? That's a bonus.
10. **The cumulative bonus value should EXCEED the core offer's stated value.** Psychologically tells the buyer the core must be insanely valuable if the bonuses alone are worth this much.
11. **Stack scarcity/urgency on the bonuses themselves** for maximum effect:
    - "Only people who sign up for [program] get [bonus] — never sold separately."
    - "If you buy today, [bonus] worth $1,000 included — gone after this week."

### 1-on-1 vs. group selling — when to deploy bonuses

**1-on-1:** ask for the sale FIRST. Don't lead with bonuses. If they say yes, *then* reveal the bonuses as a wow moment that reinforces the decision. If they say no, present the bonus that matches their stated objection — then ask again. People struggle to reject reciprocity.

**Group selling / sales pages / VSLs:** stack the bonuses upfront as part of the offer reveal. The accumulation builds momentum.

### Pro tip — bonus stack vs. core offer

If you're fulfilling everything yourself, what should be the core offer vs. what should be a bonus?

- **Core offer** = the main mechanism / promise
- **Bonus** = anything with high perceived value, especially:
  - Tools, checklists, calculators (low effort to consume)
  - Recorded workshops or trainings
  - Templates, scripts, swipe files
  - Higher levels of access for a fixed period (also doubles as upsell preview)

Bonuses that are *short but high-value* (a 1-page checklist that took you weeks to perfect) are the highest-leverage bonus material. Don't bury those inside the main course — pull them out and call them out.

### Advanced — bonuses from other people's products/services

Negotiate bonus components from adjacent businesses. They get free exposure to your customers; you get bonus stack volume at zero cost.

Pain-clinic example:
- Free 1–2 massages from a massage therapist ($150 value)
- Free chiropractic adjustments ($200 value)
- Discount/free supplements from a low-inflammation food company ($100 value)
- Discount on orthotics ($150 savings)
- Free PT session + month of pool access from a local health club ($100 value)
- Pharmacy discounts ($100/mo savings)

Total partnership-bonus value can easily exceed your core offer's price.

**Next-level move:** negotiate a referral commission for each customer who uses the bonus. Now your bonus stack is *also* a revenue stream. Hormozi's example: a $400 offer with $350 of profit on the back-end from referral commissions on the bonus partners.

This is one of the most underused tactics in the book. Worth thinking about for any offer where the avatar predictably buys adjacent things.

### Pro tip — name your bonuses with the MAGIC formula too

Run every bonus name through the MAGIC framework (`07-naming-magic.md`). A bonus called "Grocery Calculator" is half as valuable in perception as one called "5-Minute Meal Prep Genius — DIY Calculator That Saves Busy Moms 200 Hours a Year."

## Pro tip — adding bonuses instead of discounting

When negotiating to close a deal, **never discount the core offer.** Discounting teaches the customer your prices are negotiable. Add bonuses to close instead. Same financial concession to you (or less), but the customer feels they got a better deal AND you preserve pricing integrity for everyone else.

---

## Putting it together — the supply/demand math

Used in combination:
- Scarcity raises perceived value via lower supply
- Urgency forces decision timing now rather than later
- Bonuses inflate stated value without raising price

The result: same offer, dramatically more conversions, often at higher prices. And the unsatisfied demand from each campaign becomes the pent-up demand for the next.
